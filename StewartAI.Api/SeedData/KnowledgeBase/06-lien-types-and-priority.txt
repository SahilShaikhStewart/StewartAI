STEWART TITLE — LIEN TYPES, PRIORITY, AND RESOLUTION
=======================================================

WHAT IS A LIEN?
A lien is a legal claim or encumbrance on a property that serves as security for a debt or obligation. Liens must be identified and addressed during the title examination process. Unresolved liens are the #1 cause of title insurance claims.

LIEN PRIORITY RULES

General Rule: "First in time, first in right"
Liens are generally prioritized by the date and time they are recorded in the public records. The first lien recorded has the highest priority.

Exceptions to the General Rule:
1. Property tax liens: ALWAYS have first priority regardless of recording date
2. Federal tax liens: Priority from date of filing (not recording)
3. Mechanic's liens: May relate back to the date work commenced (varies by state)
4. Purchase money mortgages: May have priority over prior judgment liens in some states
5. HOA super-liens: Some states give HOA liens priority over first mortgages for limited amounts

TYPES OF LIENS

VOLUNTARY LIENS (Created by agreement)

1. Mortgage Lien / Deed of Trust
- Created when a borrower pledges property as security for a loan
- Recorded in the county where the property is located
- Released when the loan is paid in full (satisfaction/reconveyance)
- Priority: Based on recording date
- Stewart Guideline: Verify all prior mortgages are released or will be paid at closing

2. Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)
- Revolving credit line secured by the property
- May have a maximum lien amount different from the current balance
- Must be paid off and closed (not just paid down) for release
- Stewart Guideline: Obtain a payoff letter AND a closure letter for HELOCs

INVOLUNTARY LIENS (Imposed without owner's consent)

3. Property Tax Lien
- Imposed by local government for unpaid property taxes
- ALWAYS first priority — superior to all other liens
- Can result in tax sale if unpaid
- Stewart Guideline: Always verify current tax status; require payment of delinquent taxes at closing

4. Federal Tax Lien (IRS)
- Filed by the IRS for unpaid federal taxes
- Attaches to all property owned by the taxpayer
- 10-year statute of limitations from date of assessment
- 120-day right of redemption after sale
- Priority: From date of filing with the county recorder
- Stewart Guideline: Federal tax liens must be paid at closing or subordinated by the IRS

5. State Tax Lien
- Filed by state tax authority for unpaid state taxes
- Rules vary by state
- Stewart Guideline: Check state-specific requirements for release

6. Mechanic's Lien (Construction Lien)
- Filed by contractors, subcontractors, or material suppliers for unpaid work
- May relate back to the date work commenced (giving it priority over later-recorded liens)
- Filing deadlines vary by state:
  - Texas: 15th day of 4th month after work completed
  - California: 90 days after completion for direct contractors; 30 days for subcontractors
  - Florida: 90 days after final furnishing of labor/materials
  - New York: 8 months after completion for private improvements
- Stewart Guideline: For new construction, require lien waivers from all contractors and subcontractors

7. Judgment Lien
- Created when a court enters a money judgment against a property owner
- Attaches to all real property owned by the debtor in the county where recorded
- Duration varies by state (typically 5-20 years, renewable)
- Stewart Guideline: Search for judgments against all parties in the chain of title

8. HOA/Condo Association Lien
- Filed for unpaid homeowner association dues and assessments
- Some states have "super-lien" provisions giving HOA liens priority over first mortgages
- Super-lien states include: Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, DC, Hawaii, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, Washington
- Stewart Guideline: Always obtain an HOA estoppel letter showing current balance due

9. Child Support Lien
- Filed for unpaid child support obligations
- May have special priority under state law
- Stewart Guideline: Must be paid at closing or subordinated

10. Municipal Lien (Code Enforcement)
- Filed by local government for unpaid fines, code violations, or special assessments
- May include demolition liens, weed abatement liens, or utility liens
- Stewart Guideline: Obtain a municipal lien search in addition to the standard title search

11. Lis Pendens (Notice of Pending Litigation)
- Not technically a lien, but a recorded notice that litigation is pending affecting the property
- Puts all subsequent purchasers on notice of the claim
- Stewart Guideline: Do not insure over an active lis pendens without underwriter approval

LIEN RESOLUTION PROCESS

Step 1: Identify all liens during title search
Step 2: Determine lien priority
Step 3: Calculate payoff amounts (request payoff statements)
Step 4: Determine which liens will be:
  - Paid at closing (most common)
  - Released by the lienholder
  - Subordinated to the new mortgage
  - Excepted from coverage on Schedule B
Step 5: Obtain lien releases after payment
Step 6: Record lien releases

PAYOFF LETTER REQUIREMENTS
A valid payoff letter must include:
- Current balance due
- Per diem interest amount
- Good-through date
- Wire instructions for payment
- Contact information for the lienholder
- Any fees (recording fees, release preparation fees)
- Statement that a release will be provided upon payment

Stewart Guideline: Payoff letters should be no more than 30 days old at closing. For HELOCs, require both a payoff letter and account closure confirmation.

SUBORDINATION AGREEMENTS
When a new first mortgage is being placed and an existing second mortgage will remain:
- The second mortgage holder must agree to subordinate to the new first mortgage
- A subordination agreement must be recorded
- Stewart Guideline: Subordination agreements require underwriter approval for amounts exceeding $100,000
